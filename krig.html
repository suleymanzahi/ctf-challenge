Meuse-Argonne offensive
Part of the Western Front and Hundred Days Offensive of World War I

The Meuse-Argonne Offensive was a crucial Allied operation during the final stages of World War I, running from September 26 to November 11, 1918, as part of the larger Hundred Days Offensive that ended the war. It was the largest and deadliest campaign in U.S. military history, involving 1.2 million American, French, and Siamese troops and resulting in over 350,000 casualties, including 26,277 American lives. The offensive was a key engagement of the American Expeditionary Forces (AEF), commanded by General John J. Pershing, and marked the AEF's most significant contribution to the war. Despite its limited scope compared to other battles on the Western Front, the Meuse-Argonne Offensive demonstrated renewed Allied determination, especially with the support of fresh but inexperienced U.S. forces. Poor tactics, inexperience, and the Spanish flu exacerbated American losses. American forces attacked the German positions along the Meuse River and Argonne Forest, advancing toward Sedan. These actions, along with British, French, and Belgian offensives elsewhere, forced German retreats and directly contributed to the Armistice of November 11, 1918, ending the war.
